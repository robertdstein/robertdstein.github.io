About Me

I grew up in London, and did my undergraduate studies there at Imperial College. I completed my masters project in the Astroparticle group of the University of Hamburg. Since July 2017 I've moved into high-energy neutrino physics and multi-messenger astronomy. I did my PhD at DESY Zeuthen, supervised by Anna Franckowiak. I spent three years as a postdoc at Caltech working with Mansi M. Kasliwal

Now I'm the Neil Gehrels Prize Postdoctoral Fellow at JSI, based at UMD/NASA Goddard. I'm a member of two international scientific collaborations:

Zwicky Transient Facility (ZTF)

ZTF is an optical telescope on Palomar Observatory, CA, USA. ZTF images the entire northern sky every two nights, and hunts for newly-detected objects (transients) that appear between observations. ZTF has already discovered hundreds of supernovae, dozens of Tidal Disruption Events (TDEs), and many other exciting transients.

Wide-Field Infrared Transient Explorer (WINTER)

WINTER is a new infra-red telescope located at Palomar Observatory. WINTER saw first light in June 2023, and is now used to study the dynamic infra-red sky. WINTER helps us understand the properties of 'red' transients, especially those that might be dust-obscured. With WINTER we hunt for electromagnetic counterparts to gravitational waves and neutrinos, and make new discoveries in a relatively unexplored wavelength.

My research interests include:

  • High-Energy Neutrinos: We don't know exactly where most high-energy neutrinos come from, but by observing the neutrino arrival directions with telescopes, we might be able to find out. With ZTF and WINTER, I have led many of our neutrino follow-up campaigns. We successfully identified two TDEs as probable neutrino sources.

  • Gravitational-Wavse (GWs) + Gamma-Ray Bursts (GRBs): Observations of the first Binary Neutron-Star merger from LIGO revealed a spectacular kilonova AT2017gfo, detected with a variety of telescopes. With ZTF and WINTER we search for these kilonovae from the direction of GW/GRB triggers.

  • Tidal Disruption Events (TDEs): When unlucky stars passing close to supermassive black holes, the strength of tidal forces can rip the stars to shreds. This process is known as a Tidal disruption Event, and generates spectacular flares the light up the electomagnetic spectrum. In recent years, we have become increasingly adept at finding these TDEs in optical surveys.

  • Machine Learning and Big Data: We are in the era of big data, and time-domain surveys including ZTF and WINTER (and soon Rubin) detect vast numbers of candidate transients each night. I developed a novel classifier (tdescore) to analyse nuclear transients, and identify TDEs amongst the vastly more numerous contaminant AGN, stars and supernovae.

I am also enthusiastic about Science Communication and Outreach. I won the Zeuthen Science Slam (2018) and the first DESY-wide Science Slam (2019). I've also supported the annual International Cosmic Day and IceCube Masterclass outreach events for students.
